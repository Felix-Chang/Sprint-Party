## Design Context

### Users
Friend groups (2–6 people) who want to hold each other accountable on weekly tasks. Used Monday–Sunday, primarily on desktop browsers during the workday or in the evening. The user is already invested in the game because their friends are watching — the interface should reinforce that competitive energy rather than calm it down.

### Brand Personality
**Clean. Colorful. Clicky.**
Three words: *competitive, playful, crisp.*
The reference is neal.fun — generous whitespace, one clear action per screen, personality through small details not visual complexity. Layered with Mario Party's bold player colors and Duolingo's satisfying tactile feedback.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Light mode only (no dark mode for MVP)
- **Background**: Off-white (#FAFAFA) with subtle dotted texture — quiet, the content does the talking
- **Typography**: Nunito (rounded, friendly, game-like) + JetBrains Mono for invite codes and game-mechanic text
- **Colors**: White surfaces, near-black text (#1A1A2E), light gray borders. Player colors (coral, teal, yellow, purple, orange, green) are the primary expressive element — they pop hard against white
- **Anti-references**: No SaaS dashboards, no glassmorphism, no dark mode with glowing accents, no Notion/Linear vibes, no gem currencies or progress bars everywhere

### Design Principles
1. **Simplicity that feels alive** — strip away everything that doesn't serve the experience, then make what's left delightful
2. **One focus per view** — each screen has one primary action; don't combine flows
3. **Flat and crisp** — no shadows, no depth tricks, no gradients; confidence through flatness
4. **Small, fast, satisfying animations** — every animation under 300ms; feels like tactile feedback not decoration
5. **Neal.fun test** — if it would look at home on neal.fun, it belongs here; if it looks like a Figma showcase or YC demo, strip it back
